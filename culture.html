Documentation

Every room I shoot is a record, not just content.

Nightlife and culture rarely get treated as history while they're happening. I shoot DC's rooms — the openings, the residencies, the late nights — as if someone will need proof later of who built them and who was in them.

The thesis

Documentation is preservation. It's also evidence.

Scenes get written out of their own history constantly — the photographer who shot a venue's first year, the host who built its following, the stylist whose look became the room's identity, all uncredited once the venue gets popular enough to stop needing them. Good documentation is the record that keeps that from happening quietly.

It's also the economic case. Footage and photos of a packed room with a specific host or artist working it are the proof that the value in that room came from somewhere — which is exactly the evidence ATCHÉ's revenue attribution and artist commentary systems are designed to formalize. The camera work and the platform work are the same project, on two different timelines.

Where this connects

Documentation is how ATCHÉ's standards get enforced.

01

Proof of value

Coverage that shows exactly who was working a room — the host, the DJ, the photographer — becomes the raw material for revenue attribution, not just a recap reel.

02

Credibility for the certification standard

A venue claiming an ATCHÉ certification needs a visible, documented track record — consistent footage and photos are part of how that claim gets verified, not just self-reported.

03

A real archive

Years of consistent documentation mean DC nightlife and culture have an actual visual record — one that gives credit to the women who built it, long after any single event is over.
